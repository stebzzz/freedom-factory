Level 1: The Meat Market. You are 12 years old. 1570, Ming Dynasty China.

The rice has been thin for three winters. You feel your ribs through your tunic when you lie down at night, and tonight, for the first time, your father will not meet your eyes. A man with cold eyes stands at the door, a procurer, a buyer of girls. He weighs you with a glance the way butchers weigh hogs. Your father takes the sack of silver. He does not say goodbye. You are walked out into the dust road in bare feet, chained by the wrist to four other girls, and for the first time you understand that you are not a daughter anymore. You are merchandise.

The journey north lasts weeks. You sleep in carts that smell of sweat and piss. You are fed just enough rice to keep the meat on your bones. When the blood-red gates of the Forbidden City finally rise out of the mist, taller than any building you have ever imagined, you think for half a heartbeat it is a temple. It is not.

Inside, old women strip you naked in a cold stone room. They check your teeth. They check your ears, your scalp, your skin for pox scars. They part your legs without ceremony. You are a horse at market. Then they take your feet. They fold your toes under the arch until something inside snaps like green wood, and they bind them in wet cloth that will shrink as it dries. You scream. Nobody comes. Nobody ever comes. This is the beginning.

Level 2: The Viper's Nest. You are 13.

You scrub floors now. You scrub until your knuckles are raw and the stone smells like your own blood. The senior consorts glide above you in silk the color of peach blossoms, and you keep your eyes on the tiles. You have learned, already, that looking up is a mistake. A girl two beds down from yours looked up last month. She was beaten until her jaw did not sit right, and then she disappeared. Nobody asked where.

You learn the rules by watching. You learn that crying is a luxury the powerful can afford and the powerless cannot. Tears swell your eyes and dull your work and mark you as weak, and weakness in this place is a disease that ends in a silk cord or a cup of cold poison. So you swallow it. Every night, in the dark, you press your face into the straw mat and let nothing out.

You learn that a single spilled drop of tea, on the wrong sleeve, on the wrong day, is not a mistake. It is a death sentence. A girl named Mei spilled plum wine on the First Consort's hem last autumn. They found her in the well three days later. The senior women joked about it at breakfast.

You survive by becoming smaller. Invisible. You move like smoke. You memorize every consort's favorite tea, every eunuch's grudge, every hallway where the guards like to linger, every name that is safe to speak and every name that is not. Knowledge is breath in this place, and you are learning how to breathe.

Level 3: The Tightrope. You are 14.

Tonight is a banquet, and you have been chosen to pour wine. You do not know if this is a favor or a trap. In the Forbidden City there is no difference.

The hall glows with a thousand lanterns, red as arterial blood. Incense drifts across the ceiling in slow blue coils. The Emperor's mother sits at the high table. A white scar runs from her temple to her jaw, a memory, the whispers say, of the last palace purge, the one she won. She does not eat. She watches. Her eyes pass over you like a blade being dragged slowly across a whetstone.

You carry the wine jar with both hands. It is heavier than it looks. Your bound feet ache in their silk slippers, each step a small controlled fall. You reach the table of a minor noble, fat-cheeked, drunk already, leering, and he lets his hand rest on your hip as you pour. Your heart is a trapped animal inside your chest. If you flinch, you insult him. If you smile, you are immodest. If you spill, you die.

You pour. The wine meets the rim of the cup in a thin, perfect curve. You do not breathe. You do not shake. The Empress Mother's eyes find yours for half a heartbeat, and then move on. And in that half-heartbeat, you understand: you have passed. You are not useless. You are alive for at least one more season.

Level 4: The Summoning. You are 15.

The palanquin comes at midnight. Four eunuchs lift you onto it without a word. You are wrapped in a red silk robe and nothing else, that is the custom, so that nothing can be smuggled into the Emperor's chamber. A blade. A poison. A prayer.

The halls blur past in lantern light. You cross the Golden Water Bridge under a low, bone-white moon. You are set down outside a door of black lacquer and carved dragons. Inside, the Emperor is waiting.

He is older than you thought he would be. His face is smooth but his eyes are tired, the tired of a man who has watched too many people lie to him. He looks at you for a long moment, and then he says, quietly, "Please me, or you'll wish you hadn't."

Every instinct in you is screaming. Your bound feet throb. You have never been touched. But you have been watching the senior consorts for three years. You have learned how they tilt their heads. How they laugh without sound. How they make a powerful man feel, for one hour, that he is the only soul in the world.

You charm him. You pour his wine. You ask about his horses and let him talk, because men like him are starved for anyone who will listen without wanting something obvious. By dawn he is laughing. By dawn, you have a name at court. You survived the night. That is the whole prize.

Level 5: The Target. You are 16.

You are pregnant. You knew it before the physician did. And the moment your belly begins to round under your robes, you feel the eyes of every woman in the Inner Court turn on you like knives.

A consort named Lady Ling begins to smile at you. She has a jade hairpin shaped like a phoenix, and she smiles the way a cat smiles at a wounded bird. One afternoon she invites you for tea. You drink. An hour later a servant screams, Lady Ling has collapsed, foaming, poisoned, and the cup in her hand is yours.

You are dragged before the Emperor's mother. The scarred woman looks at you the way she looked at you at the banquet, two years ago. You do not cry. You do not beg. You simply tell the truth. And then, a mercy you did not earn, a loyal servant girl, one you once gave a piece of bread to in a bad winter, steps forward and swears on her life that the cups were switched.

You live. Lady Ling lives too, for now. She will keep her phoenix hairpin and her grudge.

Months later you give birth. A son. A son. The midwife weeps with relief on your behalf. You are raised to a low-ranking consort. You have a room with a window. You have a name the Emperor remembers.

You have, for the first time in your life, something to lose.

Level 6: The Purge. You are 22.

The plague comes in summer. It moves through the palace like fire through dry grass. The Emperor falls sick. The court panics. And panic, in the Forbidden City, is always paid for in blood.

A consort with a single pearl earring, Lady Xue, points her finger at you. Witchcraft, she says. She has found charms under your pillow. She has seen you burn paper dolls in the shape of the Emperor. It is a lie, every word of it. It does not matter. Lies with enough weight behind them become law.

You are dragged to the courtyard. The executioner's block is slick from the last girl. The sun is too bright. Your hair is pulled forward off your neck. The blade lifts. You close your eyes and you think of your son's small hands.

Then, footsteps. Running. Bare feet on stone. Your maid, the same one you fed in that bad winter, bursts through the guards with a stolen letter crumpled in her fist. A letter in Lady Xue's own handwriting, to a merchant, arranging the bribe. The Empress Mother reads it once. She does not speak. She only lifts one finger.

The blade falls. Not on you.

Lady Xue's pearl earring rolls across the stone, and your knees give out, and you live.

Level 7: The Embers. You are 25.

The plague spared your head but not your lungs. You cough blood into silk handkerchiefs now, and hide them in a lacquer box so no one can use them against you. Your bound feet have begun to rot inside the bandages, a sweet, terrible smell no perfume can mask. You are no longer the favorite. Younger girls walk the corridors you once walked, and the Emperor's eyes slide past you now like you are furniture.

So you do the one thing left to do. You save your son. You bribe a eunuch with the last of your jade. You send your boy, wrapped in a peasant's rags, to a distant cousin in the south, a farmer with a quiet name in a quiet village. You will never see him again. You know this. You kiss his forehead and you do not cry, because you taught yourself, at thirteen, that tears are for the powerful.

You lie now on a threadbare mat in a small room at the end of a long hall. The lantern gutters. Your breath is shallow. Girls still come to you, in secret, the new ones, the frightened ones with bound feet leaking through fresh silk. You teach them. How to pour. How to listen. How to be smoke. You are passing on the only inheritance you have, the art of surviving a world built to crush you.

You think of a dust road, and a sack of silver, and a father who could not look at you. You think of the blood-red gates. You think of the blade that did not fall. You think of your son, running somewhere far from here, free.

You outwitted death until it came knocking... and now you are history.
